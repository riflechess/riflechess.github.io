#### Meeting Info
**Type:** Public Safety Committee Meeting  
**Purpose:** Discussion regarding St. Paul Police Department (SPPD) technology usage, specifically license plate readers (LPR), drones (UAS), and real-time information systems.  
**Date/Time:** June 24, 2026, at 12:30 PM  
**Context:** The meeting featured presentations from the St. Paul Police Department regarding their current safety protocols and a presentation from the ACLU of Minnesota regarding the risks associated with automated license plate reader networks.

#### Summary
The meeting focused on how the city balances modern policing technology with constitutional rights and public privacy.

**St. Paul Police Department (SPPD) Technology Overview**
*   **Docked Drone Program:** The SPPD currently operates a drone program using "docked" units located in three different districts. These are used to provide "eyes on scene" for officers during high-priority calls. 
    *   **Performance:** Since May 30th, drones have been deployed over 500 times. In 51% of those instances, the drone arrived at the scene before a patrol car, providing crucial data for officer safety and situational awareness.
    *   **Privacy Protections:** The SPPD emphasizes that these are not used for general surveillance. Footage is only retained if it becomes part of an active investigation; otherwise, it is deleted within seven days.
*   **License Plate Readers (LPR):** The city utilizes both in-vehicle and fixed "Flock" cameras. 
    *   **Purpose:** They are strictly used for high-priority public safety matters: locating missing persons, identifying stolen vehicles, and finding wanted individuals.
    *   **Policies:** St. Paul does not use LPR data for traffic enforcement (e.g., speeding or red light tickets). Data is shared only with in-state agencies and is purged from the system within 30 to 60 days depending on the specific hardware.
*   **Real-Time Information Center:** This center helps officers make safer decisions during active incidents by providing a "common operating picture" before they arrive on the scene.

**ACLU Discussion & Points of Contention**
The discussion turned more detailed and focused when the ACLU presented regarding the "Flock" network and third-party data sharing. 

*   **The Flock Network Issue:** While SPPD maintains tight controls over their specific cameras, the ACLU highlighted that Flock is a massive national corporation with over 100,000 cameras in its network. Because it is a shared network, there are concerns regarding how easily data could be "scraped" or used by agencies outside of local control.
*   **Immigration Enforcement (287g):** A significant point of concern involved the "287g" federal agreements. Some law enforcement agencies in other parts of the state have contracts to assist with immigration enforcement. The ACLU noted that while St. Paul does not share data for these purposes, the interconnected nature of some data platforms could potentially allow local data to be accessed by entities with different priorities.
*   **Transparency Proposals:** The ACLU suggested that St. Paul move toward a "transparency portal" (similar to those used in other cities) to provide public-facing information on how often cameras are being triggered and who is accessing the data, which would help mitigate concerns about potential misuse or over-surveillance.

**Key Safety & Quality of Life Takeaways**
*   The technology has successfully helped solve cases involving missing persons and high-risk suspects in real-time.
*   The city currently maintains a "no questions asked" stance on public safety: if a tool cannot be used while protecting constitutional rights, it won't be deployed.
*   There are ongoing discussions regarding the best way to ensure that data shared with regional partners remains strictly within those agreed-upon limits.
