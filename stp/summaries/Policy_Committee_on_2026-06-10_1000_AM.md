#### Meeting Info
**Type:** Policy Committee Meeting
**Purpose:** Discussion of prison labor legislation and economic development/land use analysis.
**Date/Time:** June 10, 2026, at 10:00 AM
**Context:** The meeting included presentations from the "End Slavery in Minnesota Coalition" and a land-use analytics firm (Urban 3) regarding local tax revenue and development potential.

#### Summary
The committee discussed two primary topics during this session: the reform of prison labor systems and the economic productivity of city land.

**Prison Labor and State Legislation**
Representatives from the "End Slavery in Minnesota Coalition" presented on the current legal framework that allows for involuntary servitude as a punishment for crime. Key points included:
*   **Wage Exploitation:** The coalition highlighted that incarcerated individuals are currently paid approximately $0.25 per hour. They argue this "loophole" allows corporations to exploit cheap labor and undercuts local businesses and union power.
*   **Impact on Families:** It was noted that the system results in a significant loss of income for families (estimated at over $100 million annually across Minnesota). Furthermore, incarcerated individuals are often limited in what they can save upon release (currently capped at $500), which hinders successful reentry into the community.
*   **Proposed Solutions:** The coalition is advocating for a constitutional amendment to remove the "punishment" exception and a legislative bill to reclassify incarcerated people as workers entitled to minimum wage, better housing protections, and more robust benefits for their families.

**Economic Development and Land Use**
A presentation by Urban 3 provided a data-driven look at how land use directly affects St. Paul's tax revenue:
*   **Tax Exemptions:** The analysis revealed that St. Paul has one of the highest amounts of tax-exempt property in terms of percentage of downtown area compared to other U.S. cities. This is attributed to a high volume of state-owned land and various exemptions.
*   **Development Productivity:** The committee reviewed "productivity" metrics (dollars per acre). High-performing areas like Grand Avenue and the Downtown core were highlighted as successful examples of dense, multi-use development that generate significant property and sales tax revenue. 
*   **Proposed Strategy:** The discussion centered on "rebalancing" the city's land use portfolio by promoting more compact, mixed-use developments (the "Missing Middle") and utilizing public assets or transit corridors (like the Green Line and Snelling BRT) to increase housing volume and property tax revenue.

**Key Points of Interest for Residents:**
*   **Housing:** There is a continued push to simplify the development process to lower costs for builders while increasing the number of available housing units in St. Paul. 
*   **Public Safety/Community Impact:** The discussion on prison reform emphasized how current labor practices negatively affect local families and children by stripping away income from residents of underserved neighborhoods.
